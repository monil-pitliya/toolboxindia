Frequently Asked Questions

Is this calculator updated for FY 2025-26?

Yes! The calculator uses the latest tax slabs for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27) for both Old and New regime, including the Rs 12 lakh exemption under New regime introduced in Budget 2025, revised slab rates, and all current deduction limits. The 4% Health & Education Cess is automatically applied to the computed tax.

What deductions are supported?

Section 80C (up to Rs 1.5 lakh — PPF, ELSS, LIC, EPF, NSC, tax-saving FD), 80D health insurance (self, spouse, children + parents), HRA exemption, NPS (80CCD 1B additional Rs 50K), home loan interest (Section 24 up to Rs 2 lakh), standard deduction (Rs 75K under New Regime, Rs 50K under Old Regime), and more. Note that most deductions are only available under the Old Regime.

Which tax regime is better for me?

The calculator shows a side-by-side comparison of both regimes with the exact tax amount, take-home pay, and a clear recommendation on which regime saves you more. Generally, if your total deductions (80C + 80D + HRA + NPS + home loan) exceed Rs 3.75 lakh, the Old Regime may be better. Otherwise, the New Regime with its lower slab rates and Rs 12L exemption often wins. Enter your actual numbers to get a personalized recommendation.

Is the new regime Rs 12L exemption included?

Yes, the calculator includes all Budget 2025 changes including the Rs 12 lakh income exemption under the New Regime with marginal relief (Section 87A rebate), revised slab rates, and the Rs 75,000 standard deduction. Effectively, salaried individuals with income up to Rs 12.75 lakh pay zero tax under the New Regime.

Does it calculate take-home salary?

Yes, the calculator shows both monthly and annual take-home salary after all deductions, EPF contributions, professional tax, and income tax under both regimes. This helps you plan your monthly budget accurately and understand exactly how much lands in your bank account each month.

Which regime is better for salary above 10 lakh?

For salary above Rs 10 lakh, the answer depends entirely on your deductions. If you actively invest in PPF/ELSS (80C: Rs 1.5L), pay health insurance (80D: Rs 25-50K), claim HRA exemption, and contribute to NPS — your total deductions could exceed Rs 3.75 lakh, making the Old Regime beneficial. However, if you do not have significant deductions, the New Regime's lower slab rates (5% up to Rs 8L, 10% up to Rs 12L) combined with the Rs 12L exemption often result in lower tax. Our calculator does this exact comparison for you with your specific numbers.

How is HRA exemption calculated?

HRA (House Rent Allowance) exemption under the Old Regime is the minimum of three amounts: (1) Actual HRA received from your employer, (2) 50% of basic salary if you live in a metro city (Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, Chennai) or 40% for non-metro cities, and (3) Actual rent paid minus 10% of basic salary. For example, if your basic salary is Rs 5 lakh, HRA is Rs 2.5 lakh, and you pay Rs 15,000/month rent in Mumbai — the exemption is calculated as min(Rs 2.5L, Rs 2.5L, Rs 1.3L) = Rs 1.3 lakh. HRA exemption is not available under the New Regime.

What is the standard deduction in new regime 2025-26?

The standard deduction under the New Regime for FY 2025-26 is Rs 75,000, increased from Rs 50,000 as per Budget 2024. Under the Old Regime, the standard deduction remains Rs 50,000. This flat deduction is available to all salaried individuals and pensioners without submitting any bills or proofs. Combined with the Rs 12 lakh income exemption, salaried individuals with income up to Rs 12.75 lakh (12L + 75K standard deduction) pay zero tax under the New Regime.

Can I switch between old and new regime?

Yes. Salaried individuals can switch between Old and New regime every financial year by informing their employer at the beginning of the year (typically during the investment declaration process in April). The New Regime is the default — you must actively opt for the Old Regime if it benefits you. For individuals with business/professional income (ITR-3/4), the switch from New to Old is allowed only once in a lifetime. Use our calculator to compare both regimes every year, as changes in your salary and deductions can shift the advantage.

How are capital gains taxed?

Capital gains taxation in India (as per Budget 2024): Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) on equity shares and equity mutual funds (held less than 12 months) are taxed at 20%. Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) on equity (held over 12 months) exceeding Rs 1.25 lakh per year are taxed at 12.5%. Debt mutual fund gains are taxed at your income tax slab rate. Property LTCG (held over 24 months) is taxed at 12.5% without indexation benefit from FY 2024-25 onwards. These rates apply under both Old and New regime.

What are the tax slabs for FY 2025-26?

New Regime (default): Up to Rs 4L — Nil, Rs 4-8L — 5%, Rs 8-12L — 10%, Rs 12-16L — 15%, Rs 16-20L — 20%, Rs 20-24L — 25%, Above Rs 24L — 30%. Income up to Rs 12L is fully exempt with marginal relief via Section 87A. Old Regime: Up to Rs 2.5L — Nil, Rs 2.5-5L — 5%, Rs 5-10L — 20%, Above Rs 10L — 30%. A 4% Health & Education Cess is added on the total tax amount under both regimes. Surcharge applies for income above Rs 50 lakh.
