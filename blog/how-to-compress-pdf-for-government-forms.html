Why Are Scanned PDFs So Large?

When you scan a document using a scanner or your phone camera, the result is essentially a high-resolution photograph embedded inside a PDF. A typical phone camera captures at 12-48 megapixels, and scanning apps often save at 300 DPI or higher. This means:

  • A single-page colour scan can be 2-5 MB
  • A multi-page marksheet scan can easily reach 10-20 MB
  • Documents scanned in colour are 3-5 times larger than greyscale scans
  • PDF files created by combining multiple scanned images compound the problem

Government portals, on the other hand, were built to handle files in the 100 KB to 1 MB range. This gap between what scanners produce and what portals accept is why PDF compression is so essential for Indian students and job applicants.

Tips for Getting the Smallest Possible PDF

  1. Scan in greyscale, not colour. Unless the document requires colour (like a passport photo), greyscale scans are 3-5 times smaller. Most certificates and marksheets work perfectly in greyscale.
  2. Scan at 150-200 DPI instead of 300 DPI. For text-heavy documents, 150 DPI is perfectly readable and produces much smaller files. Use 200 DPI if you need slightly sharper results.
  3. Crop unnecessary white space. Many scanned documents have large margins that add to the file size. Crop them before converting to PDF.
  4. Compress images before creating the PDF. If you are creating a PDF from photos, first compress them using ToolBox India's Image Compressor, then convert to PDF using the Image to PDF tool.
  5. Use the multi-step approach for stubborn files. Convert PDF to images (using PDF to Images tool), compress the images, then convert back to PDF. This gives you maximum control over the final file size.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Compressing PDFs

  • Do not compress the same PDF multiple times. Each round of compression degrades quality. Start from the original scan each time you need to try a different compression level.
  • Do not use screenshots instead of scans. Screenshots are lower resolution than proper scans and may be rejected by government portals that require clear, legible documents.
  • Check the portal's exact requirements. Some portals specify file size limits in KB, others in MB. Some require PDF format, others accept only JPG. Read the notification carefully before compressing.
  • Verify after compression. Open your compressed PDF and make sure all text is readable and all details are visible. An unreadable document will be rejected during verification even if the file size is correct.
